Reading key

About the two texts

The modern academic position tends to treat the Tamil as Meykaṇḍar’s original text; the classical Śaiva Siddhānta tradition presents the sūtras as Sanskrit and āgamic in origin, with Meykaṇḍar translating and commenting on them in Tamil. This document follows the academic reading, but the reader should be aware of the debate.

How to use the layers

Technical translation — close to the structure of the text, for study. Contemplative reading (violet) — fluent English, for practice. Notes and context (collapsible) — terms, glossary, and concepts from Timalsina’s classes. Open whichever layer serves the moment.

Editorial thematic division

The sidebar themes are editorial aids, not a formal division of the text. The traditional organization of the 12 sūtras may be read differently; here the labels simply help the reader navigate the doctrinal progression.

Strict Siddhānta and Trika-integrative reading

Some contextual notes draw on Timalsina’s comparative and Trika-integrative teaching. These should not be mistaken for strict Śaiva Siddhānta doctrine. Where needed, the notes mark the difference explicitly.

Sūtra 1Causality and cycle — the opening question
Sanskrit
स्त्रीपुन्नपुंसकादित्वाज्जगतः कार्यदर्शनात् ।
अस्ति कर्ता स हृत्वैतत् सृजत्यस्मात् प्रभुर्हरः ॥ १ ॥
strīpuṃnapuṃsakāditvāj jagataḥ kāryadarśanāt | asti kartā sa hṛtvā etat sṛjati asmāt prabhuḥ haraḥ ||
Tamil
அவன் அவள் அது எனும் அவை மூவினைமையின்
தோற்றிய திதியே ஒடுங்கி மலத்து உளதாம்
அந்தம் ஆதி என்மனார் புலவர்
avan avaḷ atu enum avai mūviṉaimaiyiṉ | tōṟṟiya titiryē oṭuṅki malattū uḷatām | antam āti eṉmāṉār pulavar
Translation — Sanskrit
As the world appears in distinctions such as feminine, masculine, neuter, and others, and is perceived as an effect — there must be an agent. That Lord, Hara, having withdrawn all this into himself, emanates it from himself.
Translation — Tamil
He, she, that — these three categories, through the threefold activity of creation, preservation, and dissolution, exist within mala, without end and without beginning — so the sages declare.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
The world manifests in all its diversity. And because it is manifested, it has a source. That Lord whom we call Hara gathered the world back into himself and then brought it forth — not from outside, but from within himself.
from the Tamil
He, she, that — everything that exists moves through three motions: arising, remaining, dissolving. And this whole cycle exists within limitation, beginningless and endless. Thus taught those who saw clearly.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — Sanskrithṛtvā = “having withdrawn/recollected.” The name Hara is philosophically precise: he is the one who enfolds, the one who gathers back. asmāt = “from himself” — creation is not ex nihilo, but emanation from within Śiva. The Sanskrit emphasizes the cosmological argument: the world as effect implies an agent.
Note — Tamilmūviṉaimai = the three actions: creation (sṛṣṭi), preservation (sthiti), and dissolution (saṃhāra). The Tamil does not first ask “who created?” — it observes that the cycle exists within mala. Antam āti = without end and without beginning (anādi) — the aṇus are beginningless in Śaiva Siddhānta.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Hara
A name of Śiva as “the one who gathers back/withdraws.” It is not merely devotional; it is philosophically exact: Śiva withdraws the world into himself and emanates it from himself (sa hṛtvā etat sṛjati asmāt). This differs from the idea of an external creator.
Kartā
Agent, the one who acts. The text states asti kartā — “there is an agent” — as the conclusion of the cosmological argument.
Mala
Literally “impurity, stain.” In Śaiva Siddhānta: the ontological limitation that covers consciousness. There are three malas (see sūtra 4). The Tamil places the whole world as existing within mala — an immediate soteriological framing.
Anādi
“Beginningless.” The cosmic cycle and the individual selves (aṇus) are not created at some first moment. There is no creatio ex nihilo. This is central doctrine in Śaiva Siddhānta.

From Timalsina’s class (Class 2): “These individual aṇus have no beginning. We are not creatio ex nihilo. We are not anyone’s whimsical act.” The first sūtra establishes precisely this: the cycle exists without temporal origin, within mala. The Sanskrit version reaches the same horizon through the cosmological argument — the world as effect implies an agent — but the Tamil is more direct: the issue is not primarily cosmological; it is soteriological — the cycle is inside limitation.

Type of causality: Timalsina distinguishes three causal models. In Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika, the creator is external to the object (ārambha). In Sāṃkhya, there is real transformation (pariṇāma). In the Trika-integrative reading developed in the classes, this movement can be approached as manifestation from within oneself — without reducing Śiva to the world. In a stricter Siddhānta reading, however, the distinction between Pati, paśu, and pāśa should be preserved more strongly.

Structural difference: The Sanskrit begins from an argument — the world as effect implies a creator. The Tamil begins from a diagnosis — the cycle exists within limitation and is beginningless. They are different entrances into the same building.
Sūtra 2Śiva as distinct and non-different — karma and vyāpti
Sanskrit
अन्यस्सन्व्याप्तितोऽनन्य: कर्ता कर्मानुसारत: ।
करोति संसृतिं पुंसां आज्ञया समवेतया ॥ २ ॥
anyaḥ san vyāptitaḥ ananyaḥ kartā karmānusārataḥ | karoti saṃsṛtiṃ puṃsām ājñayā samavetayā ||
Tamil
அவையே தானேயாய் இருவினையின்
போக்கு வரவு புரிய ஆணையின்
நீக்கம் இன்றி நிற்கும் அன்றே
avaiyē tāṉēyāy iruvaṉaiyiṉ | pōkku varavu puriya āṇaiyiṉ | nīkkam iṉṟi niṟkum aṉṟē
Translation — Sanskrit
Being distinct, through vyāpti he is non-other; being agent, he acts according to the karma of individuals; he produces the transmigration of individuals through ājñā — the power of knowing and acting inherently joined to himself.
Translation — Tamil
Being those very things and also himself — in order to conduct the going and coming of karma — he remains inseparable through his command. Is it not so?
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
Śiva is distinct — and yet, through his pervasion, he is not other. He is agent — but he does not act by whim: he acts according to what each being carries. He conducts the cycle of existences through powers that are not external to him: they are what he is.
from the Tamil
Being the very things themselves and also being himself — to move the coming-and-going of karma — he remains without ever departing. Is that not so?
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — Sanskritananyaḥ = “non-other” — not merely “not separate.” “Non-other” implies identity of nature. samavetayā = “inherently conjoined” — Śiva’s powers are not external tools; they are what he is (samavāya/inherence in Nyāya vocabulary). vyāpti = pervasion — the mechanism of dīkṣā.
Note — Tamilavaiyē tāṉēyāy = “being those things and himself” — a living paradox. The final particle aṉṟē turns the sūtra into a rhetorical question: it invites the disciple to confirm for himself, not merely to hear.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Vyāpti
Pervasion, permeation. In Nyāya it is the logical relation of invariable concomitance, the basis of inference. Here it has ontological and soteriological meaning: Śiva pervades the individual. For Timalsina, vyāpti = dīkṣā — pervasion is the mechanism of initiation, not a symbolic event.
Ananyaḥ
“Non-other” — not simply “near” or “not separate,” but of the same nature. It is an affirmation of identity at the depth of apparent distinction.
Ājñā
Here not “command” in a voluntarist sense, but Śiva’s inherent power — his jñāna-śakti and kriyā-śakti acting as expression of his nature. It is qualified by samavetayā: inherently conjoined.
Samavāya
Inherence (Nyāya vocabulary): a necessary, constitutive relation, like color in a flower or heat in fire. Śiva’s powers are samavāya to him — not externally added. It differs from saṃyoga, contingent conjunction.
Saṃsṛti
The transmigratory flow, the cycle of existences. Śiva “produces the saṃsṛti of individuals” — not as punishment, but as the order that allows accumulated karma to unfold.
Karmānusārataḥ
“According to karma.” Śiva acts in accordance with the karma of each being, without arbitrariness — without vaiṣamya, partiality. Grace shines like the sun — equally for all.

Two premises in deliberate tension: Timalsina reads the verse in two structural halves. Premise 1: anyaḥ san vyāptito’nanyaḥ — being distinct, through pervasion he is non-other. Premise 2: kartā karmānusārataḥ — he is agent, but acts according to karma. Together these two theses correct two opposite errors: (a) a God who acts by arbitrary whim; (b) a consciousness identical with the world without real distinction.

Samavāya and the Śiva-śakti relation: Timalsina uses the Nyāya concept of samavāya (inherence) to explain that Śiva’s powers are not optional tools — they are what he is. This illuminates Val’s language: “there is nothing more spiritual than the body” and “power is not outside matter” — intuitions of the same logic of inherence.

Vyāpti = dīkṣā: For Timalsina, the pervasion described in this sūtra is not merely an ontological metaphor — it is the mechanism of initiation. “When you recognize that your individuality is already constituted by the five brahmamantras and the 38 divine energies — then you recognize yourself as Śiva.” Dīkṣā operates this pervasion concretely.

Two modes of the same tension: The Sanskrit formulates it analytically — “being distinct” / “being non-other.” The Tamil expresses it paradoxically: “being those things and himself.” The solution to the tension is not merely conceptual: it is pervasion — the recognition that happens in initiation.
Sūtra 3Five arguments for the existence of ātman
Sanskrit
नेतितो ममतोद्रेकादक्षोपरतिबोधतः ।
स्वापे निर्भोगतो बोधे बोद्धृत्वादस्त्यणुस्तनौ ॥ ३ ॥
netitaḥ mamatodrekāt akṣoparatibodhataḥ | svāpe nirbhogataḥ bodhe boddhṛtvāt asti aṇuḥ tanau ||
Tamil
உளது இலதென்றலின் எனதுட லென்றலின்
ஐம்புலன், ஒடுக்கம் அறிதலின், கண்படில்
உண்டிவினை யின்மையின், உண்ர்த்த உணர்தலின்
மாயா வியந்திர தனுவினுள் ஆன்மா
uḷatu ilateṉṟaliṉ eṉatuṭal eṉṟaliṉ | aimpulaṉ oṭukkam aṟitaliṉ kaṇpaṭil | uṇṭiviṉai yiṉmaiyiṉ uṇarttu uṇartaliṉ | māyā viyantira taṉuviṉuḷ āṉmā
Translation — Sanskrit
Through the experience “I am not this,” through the predominance of the sense of possession, through the knowledge that arises from the withdrawal of the senses, through the absence of cognition in deep sleep, and through being the knowing subject of that sleep upon awakening — the aṇu exists in the body.
Translation — Tamil
Because one says “it exists, it does not exist”; because one says “this is my body”; because one knows the withdrawal of the five senses; because in sleep there is no experience; and because upon awakening one is the knower of that sleep: within the body that is the instrument of māyā, the ātman exists.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
You feel: “this is not me.” You feel: “this body is mine.” You know when your senses withdraw. In deep sleep there is nothing — and yet, upon waking, you know that you slept. Something was there, in the silence without perception. That something that persists — that is the ātman in the body.
from the Tamil
You experience “exists and does not exist.” You say “my body.” You perceive when the senses close down. In sleep, experience ceases — but upon waking, you know that silence. Within the body — this machine moved by māyā — the ātman exists.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — SanskritThe strongest argument is the last: boddhṛtva — upon awakening, you know that “you knew nothing” in sleep. For that to be possible, something was present as a thread of continuity. A mere bundle of aggregates, as in a Buddhist position, cannot retrospectively cognize its own absence of cognition.
Note — Tamilmāyā viyantira taṉu = “the body that is the machine/instrument of māyā” — a unique image: the body as an automaton operated by māyā, within which the ātman exists as a distinct entity. If the body is the machine, the ātman is not the machine — and can recognize this.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Neti (netitaḥ)
“I am not this” — the experience of discrimination. Every negation requires a substrate: someone negates. This argument reflects Sāṃkhya viveka — discriminating the subject from objects.
Mamatā
The sense of possession: “my body,” “my mind.” It reveals continuity of identity through radical changes. Even if everything has changed since childhood, a line of appropriation remains.
Akṣoparati
Withdrawal (uparati) of the senses (akṣa) — as in dream. The subject persists even when the external senses are inactive.
Nirbhoga (svāpe)
Absence of experience in deep sleep (suṣupti). There is no cognition of objects — but this state is retrospectively attested.
Boddhṛtva
Being the knowing subject — upon awakening, you know that you slept. The strongest of the five arguments: for that to be possible, something persisted as a silent witness.
Aṇu
Literally “atom/the minute one” — the individual self in a state of contraction. It is not the ordinary psychological ego nor a fixed substantial entity. It is Śiva under delimitation by the malas. For Timalsina: “aṇutā = forgetting, not punishment.”

The implicit opponent: The sūtra answers the Buddhist position of anātman (non-self), in which “I” would be only a bundle (skandha) of aggregates with no core. The five arguments show that this model does not explain the continuity of identity across states. Timalsina: “a bundle of aggregates cannot retrospectively cognize its own absence of cognition.”

Aṇu vs. Sāṃkhya Puruṣa: The Śaiva aṇu is not identical to the Sāṃkhya Puruṣa. Puruṣa is pure passive witness. The aṇu has powers of knowing and acting — but these powers are suspended by the malas. The difference is that in Śaivism the self can be recognized as Śiva; in Sāṃkhya, Puruṣa remains eternally separate from Prakṛti.

Connection with practice: You told Val: “we are a contraction of that.” She immediately confirmed it. Sūtra 3 is the textual basis for that intuition: the aṇu exists as contracted power — Śiva in a mode of forgetting, not a being of another species.

Logic and phenomenology: The Sanskrit chains the five arguments logically. The Tamil chains them phenomenologically — as signs of lived experience. The Tamil’s exclusive image — body as machine of māyā — has doctrinal weight: it anticipates the soteriology. If the body is the instrument, the ātman is not the instrument.
Sūtra 4The ātman and the antaḥkaraṇa — king and ministers
Sanskrit
आत्मान्तःकरणादन्योऽप्यन्वितो मन्त्रिभूपवत् ।
अवस्थापंचकस्थ: स्यान्मलरुद्धस्वदृक्क्रिय: ॥ ४ ॥
ātmā antaḥkaraṇāt anyaḥ api anvitaḥ mantribhūpavat | avasthāpañcakasthaḥ syāt malaruddhasvadṛkkriyaḥ ||
Tamil
அந்தக் கரணம் அவற்றின் ஒன்றன்று,
சந்தித்தது ஆன்மா சகசமலத் துணராது
அமைச்சுஅரசு ஏய்ப்பநின்று அஞ்சவத் தைத்தே
antakkaraṇam avaṟṟiṉ oṉṟaṉṟu | cantittatū āṉmā sakacamalat tuṇarātu | amaiccaracu ēyppa niṟṟu añcavat taitē
Translation — Sanskrit
The ātman, distinct from the internal apparatus and yet associated with it — like a king associated with his ministers — is situated in the five states; its own powers of knowing and acting are suspended by the malas.
Translation — Tamil
The internal apparatus is not one object among others; the ātman, associated with it, does not know its true nature because of the innate mala — remaining as minister and king — in the five states.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
The ātman is not the mind, not the intellect, not the ego — and yet it dwells with all of them, as a king dwells with his ministers. Present in all states — waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and beyond. But its power to see and act is veiled by the limitations that surround it.
from the Tamil
The internal apparatus is not an object — it is the instrument. The ātman, associated with it, does not know itself because mala is innate: it was born with it; it was not acquired. Like a king among ministers — present, but covered — in the five states.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — Sanskritdṛk = jñāna-śakti (power of knowing); kriyā = kriyā-śakti (power of acting) — suspended by the malas. The antaḥkaraṇa includes manas (mind), buddhi (intellect), citta (sentience), and ahaṅkāra (ego). Without the ātman, they lose function — like ministers without a king.
Note — Tamilsakaca mala = innate, co-born mala (sahaja). The Tamil specifies that āṇava mala was not accumulated through action — it is the constitutive condition of the paśu. The Sanskrit only says “blocked by mala” without this specification.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Antaḥkaraṇa
The “internal instrument” of cognition — mind (manas), intellect (buddhi), sentience (citta), and ego (ahaṅkāra). It is the Sāṃkhya inheritance that Śaivism incorporates and reinscribes.
The three malas
Ontological limitations of the aṇu: āṇava mala limits being — “I feel small and separate”; māyīya mala limits knowing — “I perceive the world as fragmented”; kārma mala limits acting — “I act compulsively.” They correspond to the contracted forms of Śiva’s three powers: icchā-śakti, jñāna-śakti, kriyā-śakti.
Āṇava mala (sahaja)
The most primordial limitation — “innate, co-born.” It is not accumulated through action; it is the very condition of being an aṇu. The Tamil uses sakaca mala to emphasize this constitutive character.
Avasthā-pañcaka
The five states of consciousness: jāgrat (waking), svapna (dream), suṣupti (deep sleep), turīya (the fourth), turyātīta (beyond the fourth). The ātman is “situated” in them. Siddhānta treats it as an actor traversing states; Trika treats turīya as the ground that pervades the first three.

The preserved Sāṃkhya inheritance: The distinction between antaḥkaraṇa and ātman is directly inherited from Sāṃkhya — Puruṣa separate from buddhi/ahaṃkāra/manas. Timalsina: Sāṃkhya is the “first step” — “there is almost nothing the Sāṃkhyas teach that we need to reject; we only need to learn more.” This sūtra shows Śaivism preserving Sāṃkhya’s discriminative gain and reinscribing it into a richer ontology.

In samādhi: When the ātman withdraws from engagement with the antaḥkaraṇa, the ministers lose function: the mind no longer mentalizes, the intellect no longer judges, the ego no longer functions. This is what happens in samādhi — not that the ātman leaves the body, but that the relation of engagement with the cognitive apparatus is temporarily suspended.

Siddhānta/Trika difference regarding the states: Timalsina marks it explicitly: in Siddhānta, the self passes through the five states like an actor passing through stations. In Trika (Śivasūtra III.20: triṣu caturthaṃ tailavad āsecyam), the fourth state must be “poured like oil through the three” — it is the ground permeating all, not a separate station. This difference is not merely terminological; it is ontological.

The king and ministers metaphor: Both versions use the same image. The Tamil adds that mala is innate — and this changes the soteriology: it is not only a matter of removing something accumulated, but of recognizing and dissolving a constitutive contraction.
Sūtra 5The senses do not know themselves — the magnet and the iron
Sanskrit
विदन्त्यक्षाणि पुंसार्थान्न स्वयं सोऽपि शंभुना ।
तद्विकारी शिवश्चेन्न कान्तोऽयोवत्स तं नयेत् ॥ ५ ॥
vidanti akṣāṇi puṃsārthān na svayam saḥ api śaṃbhunā | tad vikārī śivaḥ cet na kāntaḥ ayovat sa taṃ nayet ||
Tamil
விளம்பிய உள்ளத்து மெய்வாய் கண் மூக்கு
அளந்தளந்து அறியா ஆங்கவை போலத்
தாம் தம் உணர்வில் தமியருள்
காந்தங் கண்ட பசாசத்து அவையே
viḷampiya uḷḷattu meyvāy kaṇ mūkku | aḷantalantu aṟiyā āṅkavai pōla | tām tam uṇarvilh tamiyaruḷ | kāntaṅ kaṇṭa pacācattu avaiyē
Translation — Sanskrit
The senses perceive objects for the individual — not by themselves; even he perceives through Śambhu. Śiva is not altered by this — like the magnet moves iron, he conducts the individual toward himself.
Translation — Tamil
Just as skin, mouth, eyes, and nose — measuring and measuring their objects — do not know by themselves in the field of mind: likewise, through their own consciousness, the senses alone move like the iron that saw the magnet.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
The senses perceive — but not on their own. The individual himself perceives — but not on his own. It is Śiva who illumines all this, without being altered in the process. Like the magnet that attracts iron without becoming iron — Śiva draws the individual toward himself.
from the Tamil
Skin, mouth, eyes, nose — they measure and measure, but do not come to know by themselves. Through their own consciousness, the senses move alone — like the iron that saw the magnet and was drawn by what it saw.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — Sanskritkāntaḥ = magnet, “the attractive one” — a technical usage, not “beloved.” The magnet does not become iron; Śiva is not altered by the relation. The attraction is real, but the nature of the attractor remains intact — Śiva’s impassibility.
Note — Tamilkāntaṅ kaṇṭa pacācu = “the iron that saw the magnet” — the verb is one of seeing (kaṇṭa). The soul is not merely attracted; it sees Śiva and is moved. There is recognition in the attraction. aḷantu aḷantu = “measuring and measuring” — emphatic repetition: however much the senses measure, they do not attain self-knowledge.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Akṣāṇi
The senses — the instruments of perception. They perceive for the individual, but not by themselves: they depend on the ātman, which in turn depends on Śiva to be illumined.
Kāntaḥ / ayovat
Magnet / like iron. The central metaphor: Śiva attracts the individual without effort and without being altered — like a magnet that does not need to “try” to attract iron.
Prakāśa
Luminosity, the illumination that makes objects knowable. It is implicit here: Śiva is the prakāśa that illumines the ātman, which in turn illumines the senses.

The chain of illumination: This sūtra establishes the chain that sūtra 11 will complete: Śiva illumines the ātman → the ātman illumines the senses → the senses perceive objects. Without Śiva, the ātman does not know itself; without the ātman, the senses do not function. The devotion requested in sūtra 11 is the logical consequence of this chain, not merely an emotional impulse.

Śaktipāta as attraction: The magnet metaphor appears in Timalsina in the context of śaktipāta: “grace shines equally — like the sun. What varies is the degree of openness of the receiver.” The magnet is always present; the iron — the practitioner — must be in a position to be drawn. Initiation (dīkṣā) creates that position.

Tamil: the dimension of recognition: The Tamil says “the iron that saw the magnet,” not merely “the iron attracted by the magnet.” This small difference matters: in the Tamil, attraction has a component of seeing/recognition. As a comparative reading, it resonates with pratyabhijñā, recognition, central to Trika.

Attraction and seeing: The Sanskrit emphasizes Śiva’s agency as an impassible mover. The Tamil adds that the iron sees the magnet — there is recognition in attraction, not only mechanics. In the Tamil, grace has a component of mutual perception.
Sūtra 6The nature of Śiva — beyond the two extremes
Sanskrit
अदृश्यं चेदसद्भावो दृश्यं चेज्जडिमा भवेत् ।
शंभोस्तद्व्यतिरेकेण ज्ञेयं रूपं विदुर्बुधा: ॥ ६ ॥

Note — SanskritA classic epistemological dilemma: Śiva cannot be an object of sensory perception — he would be jaḍa, inert — nor absolutely imperceptible — he would be asat, non-existent. tadvyatirekeṇa = “transcending that pair” — a third category escaping the dichotomy.
Note — Tamilciva-cat = the conscious being/existence of Śiva — not merely “existent” in the logical sense, but living and knowing existence. The Tamil conclusion frames the issue with more explicit ontological emphasis than the Sanskrit.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Jaḍa
Inert, without consciousness — the opposite pole of conscious (cit). If Śiva were perceptible as an object, he would be jaḍa, reduced to material thing.
Asat
The non-existent, that which has no being. If Śiva were absolutely imperceptible, the conclusion would be that he does not exist — asat.
Civa-cat (Tamil)
“The being of Śiva” — sat as conscious existence, not merely logical existence. This is ontologically more explicit than the Sanskrit’s epistemological formulation.

Viśvottīrṇa and viśvamaya: This sūtra prepares the ground for what Timalsina developed in the introductory class: Śiva as viśvottīrṇa (transcending all manifestation) and viśvamaya (being all manifestation) — simultaneously, not alternately. This escapes the pair perceptible/imperceptible: Śiva is not “outside” the world as pure invisibility, nor reduced to the world as pure visibility.

Comparative Trika reading: In Advaita Vedānta, the world is often read as māyā in a strong sense, whereas in Trika the world is real as expression of Śiva (viśvamaya). This sūtra can be used as a comparative bridge: Śiva is neither reduced to a perceptible object nor to unknowable absence. In a stricter Siddhānta reading, this transcendence should be preserved without erasing the distinction between Śiva and the soul.

Epistemology and ontology: The Sanskrit concludes with an epistemological category — “knowable form.” The Tamil concludes with an ontological category — “the being of Śiva.” They reach the same point from different angles.
Sūtra 7The ātman as third — beyond the conscious and the inert
Sanskrit

Tamil
யாவையும் சூனியம் சத்து எதிர் ஆகலின்
சத்தே அறியாது அசத்து இலது அறியாது

Translation — Sanskrit
The inert does not know in the presence of consciousness; nor is pure consciousness known by the inert; the two do not know each other mutually. The knower of both the world and Śiva — that ātman is distinct from both.
Translation — Tamil
All things would be void — for consciousness stands before them; pure consciousness alone does not cognize objects; the inert does not know, for cognition is lacking in it. The ātman, which knows both types, is what is reduced to neither.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
The stone does not know the consciousness that illumines it. Śiva’s pure consciousness is not known by the inert. Neither knows the other mutually. But there is something that knows the world and knows Śiva — that is the ātman, which is reduced to neither.
from the Tamil
Without the ātman as ground, everything would be void — because consciousness is always present before things. But pure consciousness alone does not cognize objects; and the inert cognizes nothing. The ātman is what knows both — and therefore is neither one.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — SanskritThe ātman as an irreducible third principle: not the inert (acit), not Śiva’s pure consciousness (cit). It knows both the world (prapañca) and Śiva — therefore it must be distinct from both.
Note — TamilThis edition reads the verse as an anti-śūnyavāda argument — a refutation of voidness as ultimate reality. “All things would be void” functions as the consequence of denying the ātman, not as an affirmation of śūnyatā. The logical direction: sat prevents śūnya.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Acit / Cit
The central pair of Śaiva ontology: acit = the inert, without consciousness (Prakṛti in Sāṃkhya); cit = pure consciousness (Puruṣa in Sāṃkhya, Śiva in Trika). The Śaiva Siddhānta ātman is the third — neither acit nor Śiva’s full cit.
Vettā
The knower. The ātman is vettā of both the world (prapañca) and Śiva. This places it in a position that neither of the other two occupies.
Śūnyavāda
The Buddhist position that ultimate reality is void (śūnya). The Tamil refutes this position: without the ātman as synthesis, objects would dissolve into void, which is absurd in this argument.
Svabhāva
Intrinsic nature, basis. Timalsina notes in Q&A that change implies some basis — without some svabhāva, the very idea of change lacks ground. The ātman is this ground without being a rigid substance.

The third principle of Śaiva Siddhānta: Sāṃkhya has two principles: Puruṣa (consciousness) and Prakṛti (nature). Śaiva Siddhānta has three: Pati (Śiva/cit), Paśu (ātman/the third), and Pāśa (the inert and the malas). Sūtra 7 is the epistemological justification for this third: it exists because it alone knows both the world and Śiva — a position none of the other two occupies.

Dialogue with Buddhism — Q&A from Class 2: Timalsina recognizes the convergence between Buddhist ālaya-vijñāna and Śaiva karmic ontology, but introduces the turning point: “Change implies some kind of basis or potential. If everything changes, there is something that is being said to ‘change.’ That something need not be a rigid substance, but it also cannot be treated as absolute nothing.” The ātman is this ground — more than Buddhist śūnya, less than a rigid self-substance.

Two paths to the same third: The Sanskrit reaches the ātman through the logic of mutual non-knowing. The Tamil reaches it through the refutation of voidness: without the ātman as synthesis, everything would dissolve into nothing. The ātman is what prevents śūnya.
Sūtra 8Guru and awakening — the hunters of the senses

Translation — Sanskrit
Instructed by the guru with the words “dwelling among the afflictions of the senses, you do not know yourself” — having abandoned these afflictions through the guru, the blessed one, now distinct, reaches that supreme state.
Translation — Tamil
“You grew confused among the hunters of the five senses” — the guru, being himself the primordial Guru, having instructed through tapas, and the disciple having abandoned the senses: because there is no alterity, the ātman goes to the feet of Hara.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
The guru says: “You grew up surrounded by the afflictions of the senses — and because of that, you do not know yourself.” Through this instruction, the disciple abandons them. Freed from them, different from what he was, the blessed one reaches that state which was always available.

Note — Sanskritvyādha = affliction/disease of the senses. Abandonment is not suppression — it is the recognition that the senses are not the subject. anyaḥ = “distinct” — the liberated one is distinct from the identification with the antaḥkaraṇa described in sūtra 4.

Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Guru (tammutal kuruvumāy)
“Being himself the primordial guru.” In the Tamil, the human guru is not merely a representative of the Ādiguru: he is the Ādiguru acting. Śiva as Dakṣiṇāmūrti — the silent guru holding the deer — manifests through the human guru. Identity, not mediation.
Tapas
Spiritual heat, contemplative effort. In the Tamil, the guru instructs “through tapas” — not only through speech, but through transmission that comes from realized practice. This is what distinguishes an ācārya (śrotriya + brahmaniṣṭha) from an instructor.
Ananyam iṉmai (Tamil)
Absence of alterity. Liberation is possible because separation is not absolute; there is a relation of pervasion between the ātman and Śiva. This retrieves the principle of sūtra 2 (ananyaḥ) in the direction of practice.

Dīkṣā as the will of rediscovery: Timalsina in the Q&A of Class 2: “Real dīkṣā is Śiva’s will to rediscover his identity through the individual. When does that happen? When the individual has the impulse to recognize his true Self.” The instruction of the guru described in this sūtra is the catalyst — not the cause, but what keeps the direction alive.

Criteria for the guru — from the classes: Timalsina cites the Kulārṇavatantra: without mutual examination, guru and disciple become piśācas. The guru must be śrotriya (embodying śāstra) and brahmaniṣṭha (established in practice). The Tamil “instructed through tapas” captures precisely brahmaniṣṭha: transmission comes from lived realization, not erudition.

Sāṃsiddhikas: Timalsina mentions rare cases of realization without human mediation. “We should not project ourselves into the shadow of Ramaṇa Maharṣi. It can happen, but it is absolutely rare.” Sūtra 8 describes the ordinary path — and it passes through the guru.

Hunters and afflictions: The Sanskrit uses “afflictions of the senses.” The Tamil uses “hunters of the senses” — a more vivid image: the senses as predators that capture the ātman. The Tamil also specifies that the guru is the Ādiguru — not merely his instrument.
Sūtra 9The eye of knowledge and the pañcākṣarī
Sanskrit
चिद्दशात्मनि दृष्ट्वेशं त्यक्त्वा वृत्तिमरीचिकाम् ।
लब्ध्वा शिवपदच्छायां ध्यायेत्पंचाक्षरीं सुधी: ॥ ९ ॥

Tamil
ஊனக்கண் பாசம் உணராப் பதியை ஞானக் கண்ணினில் சிந்தை நாடி
உராத்துனைத்தேர்த்து எனப் பாசம் ஒருவத்
தண்ணிழலாம் பதி, விதி எண்ணும் அஞ்செழுத்தே

Translation — Sanskrit
Having perceived the Lord in the ātman as pure consciousness, having abandoned the mirage of mental modifications, having attained the shelter of the state of Śiva — the intelligent one meditates on the five-syllabled mantra.
Translation — Tamil
The Lord whom the eye of flesh cannot know — seeking him in the mind through the eye of knowledge, rubbing and discerning with precision so as to loosen the bond — recite the five syllables prescribed by the Lord who is cool shade.
Contemplative reading
from the Sanskrit
Perceive the Lord in the very ātman — as pure consciousness. Abandon the mirage of mental modifications, which seem real but are like water in the desert. Find the shade of Śiva. And then, in that shelter, meditate on the five syllables.
from the Tamil
The eye of flesh does not see the Lord. But the eye of knowledge sees. Seek him with the mind. Rub, as though kindling fire by friction — discover with clarity. And thus the bond loosens. Then recite the five syllables — prescribed by the Lord who is cool shade in the heat.
Textual notes — Sanskrit · Tamil
Note — Sanskritvṛtti-marīcikā = “the mirage of vṛttis/mental modifications” — the vṛttis as the illusion of an oasis in the desert. śivapadachāyā = “under the shade of Śiva’s state” — protection and refuge. Meditation on the pañcākṣarī comes after recognition — it consolidates, not precedes.
Note — Tamilurāttu naittērttu = “rubbing and discerning” — fire by friction: knowledge of Śiva arises through intense friction, not gentle abandonment. taṇṇiḻalām pati = “the Lord who is cool shade” — a climatic metaphor from South India: real and urgent relief under the scorching sun.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Pañcākṣarī
The five-syllabled mantra: na-ma-śi-vā-ya (Namaḥ Śivāya). Central to Śaiva Siddhānta. The full form with Namaḥ has six syllables; the pañcākṣarī is the five-syllable core. It comes after recognition — not as prerequisite, but as consolidation.
Vṛtti
Mental modification — movements of the mind that create the appearance of objects as independent entities. Vṛtti-marīcikā = the mirage of these modifications, like an oasis in the desert that is not there.
Jñāna-kaṇ (Tamil)
“The eye of knowledge” — opposed to ūṉa-kaṇ, the eye of flesh. The Tamil articulates the difference between sensory perception and contemplative vision with imagistic clarity.
Āṇavopāya / Śāktopāya
The upāyas or means in Trika. Meditation on the pañcākṣarī is āṇavopāya — the technical path through body and sound. The recognition (ciddaśātmani dṛṣṭvā īśam) that precedes it is śāktopāya — the path of refined cognition.

Mantra not as psychological tool: Timalsina in the introductory Q&A: “The mantra is not a psychological tool of concentration — it is participation in the phonetic structure of reality. The difference between using a mantra and inhabiting a mantra is the difference between āṇavopāya and śāktopāya.” The pañcākṣarī described in this sūtra is the most concrete form of Śiva’s mantric body being transmitted to the practitioner.

The order of practice: The sūtra is explicit about the sequence: (1) perceive Śiva in the ātman; (2) abandon the mirage of vṛttis; (3) reach the shelter of Śiva; (4) meditate on the pañcākṣarī. The mantra comes at the end, not at the beginning. This is coherent with Timalsina’s teaching: “do not be a collection of maps — be the expedition.” Technique consolidates recognition; it does not replace it.

Friction vs. abandonment: The Sanskrit describes release from vṛttis as abandoning a mirage. The Tamil describes it as rubbing — intense friction that produces fire and clarity. The quality of effort differs; the destination — Hara as cool shade — is the same.

Tamil

The siddha who has attained unity with Śiva — dependent on That, dwelling in the space of consciousness — becomes untouched by mala, māyā, and the rest, bearer of direct self-experience.

Having become He himself — on that path, becoming one, remaining in service to the Lord — mala, māyā, and powerful karma no longer exist.

from the Tamil
Having become He himself, on that path — becoming one and remaining in service to the Lord — mala, māyā, karma: all of that ceases to exist. Liberation is not absence of relation. It is relation transformed into service.

Note — Tamiliṟaippaṇi niṟka = “remaining in service to the Lord” — absent from the Sanskrit, central to Śaiva Siddhānta: even after realization, service (sevā) remains. Liberation does not dissolve all relation — it transforms it.
Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Siddha
The one who has realized (gata-siddha = one who has reached accomplishment). In Val’s vocabulary: the progression paśu → siddha → vīra → yogī. Here “siddha” describes the condition of unity with Śiva.
Svānubhūti
Direct self-experience — not conceptual knowledge about the Self, but direct experience of the Self. It distinguishes realization from philosophical erudition.
Sevā (iṟaippaṇi)
Service to the Lord — the condition of the liberated one in Śaiva Siddhānta. This distinguishes Siddhānta from Advaita Vedānta: in Advaita liberation dissolves all distinction; in Siddhānta the liberated one remains in service — relation transformed, not eliminated.
Kha-vṛttika
“Whose function is in ākāśa/space.” The liberated one dwells in the openness of consciousness. Ākāśa here is not physical space, but the opening of unbounded consciousness.

Jīvanmukta — liberation in life: Timalsina on Abhinavagupta: the jīvanmukta “moves freely in the world” — he does not withdraw from it. “The concept of freedom shifts life from suffering to play.” Sūtra 10 describes this state: untouched by the malas, yet present and in service. It is not indifference — it is action without residue.

Turīyātīta as return to the world: Timalsina in the Q&A of Class 2: “Turīyātīta is not an intensification of negation — it is the reaffirmation of the world after transcendence has been recognized.” The siddha of sūtra 10 does not live in the abstraction of permanent turīya — he inhabits the world (kha-vṛttika) with the realized state. The world returns. Form returns. Action returns — but as service, not compulsion.

Karmic roasting process: Q&A from Class 2: “Dīkṣā inaugurates a process of ‘roasting’ — the gradual burning of karmic material, the reduction of the viscosity of attachment. The problem is not action; it is adhesion to action.” Sūtra 10 is the state where this burning has reached completion: mala, māyā, and powerful karma “no longer exist” — they were not suppressed; they were consumed by realization.

Dissolution and service: The Sanskrit describes the untouchedness of the liberated one. The Tamil adds that the liberated one remains in service. Liberation in Siddhānta is not indifference; it is love transformed into action without residue.

Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Darśayitā
The revealer, the one who makes seeing possible. The ātman is darśayitā of the senses; Śiva is darśayitā of the ātman. The term establishes a hierarchy of illumination.
Parā bhakti
Supreme devotion — not as religious emotion, but as fundamental orientation of being toward its source. In the context of the sūtra, it is the response to an understanding, not an arbitrary feeling.
Ayarā aṉpin (Tamil)
“Love that does not faint/does not forget” — the specific quality of devotion prescribed by the Tamil. It implies continuity through the highs and lows of practice, without depending on peak states.
Prakāśa-vimarśa (Trika)
The two inseparable aspects of consciousness: prakāśa (luminosity, illumination) and vimarśa (reflexive self-cognition). This sūtra describes the chain of prakāśa — Śiva illuminating the ātman — which Trika radicalizes: Śiva not only illumines, but is consciousness knowing itself.

Chain of illumination and Prakāśa-vimarśa: Sūtra 11 describes Śiva as illuminator of the ātman. In Trika, Timalsina goes further: “Prakāśa-vimarśa — two inseparable modes of consciousness. Prakāśa = luminosity; vimarśa = reflexive self-cognition. Consciousness without vimarśa would be inert like matter.” Siddhānta preserves the functional distinction between Śiva and ātman as illuminator and illumined; Trika interprets this relation, at the ultimate level, as consciousness knowing itself.

Bhakti as logical consequence: This sūtra does something rare in doctrinal texts: it converts a philosophical conclusion into a devotional imperative. “Therefore... one should cultivate parā bhakti.” The logic is: if Śiva is the ground without which you cannot know anything — including yourself — then total orientation toward Śiva is not merely a religious choice; it is a response to the structure of reality. Timalsina: “Surrender, surrender, surrender to Lord Śiva — and that higher being is inside you, in the form of you.”

Chain and unwavering love: The Sanskrit reaches devotion through the logic of ontological dependence. The Tamil reaches it through the metaphor of mutual revelation — and specifies love as something that does not forget. Heart and argument meet at the same point, in different registers.

Context and glossary — concepts from the classes
Sūtra glossary
Śivālaya
The temple of Śiva — not only a physical space, but a consecrated space in which the form of Śiva is present. In the Tamil, the form of realized practitioners takes on the same sacred quality.
Veṣa
Form, appearance — here the form of saints and realized devotees. By venerating them, the practitioner venerates Śiva in human form.
Mālaṟa nēyam
“Love free from mala/delusion” — lucid love, not blind love. The devotee who reaches sūtra 12 loves with clarity, without distortion by the malas.
Śaivārtha-nirṇaya
“The determination/resolution of the Śaiva meaning.” The text presents itself not as a speculative treatise but as a definitive resolution. Śivajñānabodha is not merely an introduction to Śaivism — it is its essential formulation in 12 sūtras.

The community as vehicle of the sacred: The Tamil closes the text with its most radical statement: the form of realized devotees is venerated as Hara. This is a theology of saṅgha: the community of practitioners is not merely social support for the path, but a living expression of the sacred. Timalsina on the Vimarsha Foundation: the gurukula where seniors mentor beginners without bureaucracy — a structure based on this same logic.

The complete cycle of Pati-Paśu-Pāśa: The text began in sūtra 1 with the cycle within mala (pāśa). It then established the relations between Pati, paśu, and pāśa. It closes in sūtra 12 with the purified devotee being venerated as bearer of Śiva’s presence — without the Siddhāntic doctrinal distinction between the Lord and the soul needing to be erased.

Śaivārtha-nirṇaya — not speculation, but resolution: The closing declares the text’s own function. Timalsina on choosing this text for the course: “short and strategic texts — see what they say, how they argue, how they assemble the ground.” Śivajñānabodha does not invite indefinite philosophical debate — it resolves. In this editorial reading, twelve sūtras cover diagnosis, metaphysics, and therapeutics.

The Tamil’s more radical closing: The Sanskrit closes with temple and form of the saints. The Tamil goes further: the very form of realized devotees is venerated as Hara. The cycle closes with the community of devotees appearing as a living place of Śiva’s presence — while, in a Siddhāntic reading, preserving the distinction between the Lord and the purified soul.

General notes for study

On textual priority: The modern academic position generally treats the Tamil as the original text of Meykaṇḍar, while the classical Śaiva Siddhānta tradition presents the sūtras as Sanskrit and āgamic in origin, with Meykaṇḍar translating and commenting on them in Tamil. This edition follows the academic reading, but the reader should keep the traditional framing in view.

On the thematic map: The division used here — causality, karma, proof of the ātman, guru, mantra, liberation, community — is editorial. It is meant to make the study easier; it is not presented as the formal structure of the source text.

Strict Siddhānta vs. Trika-integrative reading: Śaiva Siddhānta and Trika share a common Śaiva field and many conceptual bridges, but they are not identical. Siddhānta preserves more strongly the distinction between Pati, paśu, and pāśa; Trika radicalizes recognition into ultimate identity with Śiva. The contextual notes sometimes use Timalsina’s comparative lens; those passages should be read as interpretive support, not as a collapse of the two systems.

Important Tamil contributions: Several Tamil images are not captured fully by the Sanskrit: the body as māyā viyantira taṉu, the machine/instrument of māyā (sūtra 3); the senses as vēṭar, hunters (sūtra 8); and Śiva as taṇṇiḻal, cool shade (sūtra 9). These are not decorative metaphors only; they carry experiential and doctrinal weight.

Two critical cautions: In sūtra 1, antam āti should be read here as “without end and without beginning,” not “having beginning and end.” In sūtra 7, the Tamil is read here as anti-śūnyavāda: “all things would be void” if the ātman were denied, not as an affirmation that things simply are void before the Real.
